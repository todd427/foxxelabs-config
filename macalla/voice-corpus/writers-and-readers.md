Writers And Readers

Fandom. Canon.

As a writer, I want to create stories in worlds that interest me. I love doing that. In fact, I love it so much that I want to share the stories with others. I also want the flexibility to tell different stories.

As a reader, I love it when someone presents me with great characters, a great world, and a marvelous story. I remember diving back into The Hobbit when I finished The Lord Of The Rings over and over again, because I just had to get back into the world. I remember being entranced with Lloyd Alexander’s characters in The Book Of Three and the rest of the Chronicles of Prydain. 

I read Dune over and over, so much so that even now, decades after my last read, I knew when they were using exact dialog in the new film and when they’d dropped/altered scenes (like the whole thing about Paul’s Mentat training). 

And, of course, I remember reading Weyr Search when it came out in Analog and then reading the slightly longer version when it became the first part of Dragonflight. Certain images/passages from that book still move me just thinking about them today. Like Lessa throwing the collar off the dead watch-wher. Or Lessa’s sudden remorse when she was forced to realize that she wasn’t the only one who had suffered from Fax. Or the heart-rending scene with F’lar and Robinton when Robinton tells F’lar he must send to Southern for Kylara.

And those are only some of the thousands of books I’ve read in my life.

	When we write, we are pouring our hearts into our words. We’re also condensing stories from our emotions, thoughts, images, and feelings into clumsy, awkward words.

	A book is a transformation; and it transforms the writer first.

	It transforms the reader, as well. But the story also is transformed by the reader. Because the reader brings their experiences, their senses, their feelings, and their emotions to the book. It becomes theirs.

	That’s as it should be; the worlds that authors create with their words become transformed into the worlds that readers create in their minds.

	Readers who adore certain worlds become ardent fans of those worlds. And that’s not only completely understandable, it’s what writers want.

	There’s a duality here -- it’s a writer’s work, it’s a reader’s pleasure. The book, when read, becomes a part of the reader, just as it was a part of the writer.

	That duality can (and often does) create problems.  Who owns the book? Well, the writer owns the words on the paper.  But, I’d say, the reader owns the world they built when they read the words off the paper. Just as the act of writing transforms an idea into a reality (words on page), so does the act of reading transform a reality (the words on the page) into an idea (what the reader gets out of the book).

	So I think we need to recognize this and respect it.  That the writer builds worlds and stories in their minds, translates them into static words on a page, and the reader takes those words and creates their own worlds and stories in their minds. 

It’s a collaborative process. 

It’s one of the many marvelous ways in which we share our lives with each other.
