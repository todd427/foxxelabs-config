## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Executive summary</span>

A new academic discipline is forming around the practice of directing AI systems against fuzzy professional goals. The discipline has its own conceptual vocabulary, its own pedagogical model, and its own characteristic failure modes. It will become foundational to professional education at every level within ten years, and a small number of institutions will be recognised internationally as having defined its shape. The window in which any institution can join that group is approximately three years.

ATU sits in an unusual position. The foundational design work for the discipline's first credentialed programmes — a four-year undergraduate minor, a three-course Level 9 Postgraduate Certificate, a hybrid MSc, and a doctoral programme — is already authored and ready for institutional development. The pedagogical model has been thought through, the case library and pattern catalogue have begun emerging, and an institutional home capable of moving at the speed the moment requires exists at ATU's scale of operation.

This document sets out the trajectory the discipline is following over the next decade, names what ATU has already, identifies the artefacts and institutional commitments required to convert current portfolio work into a defining position, and presents the decision points the next eighteen months must address.

The strategic question is not whether to teach an AI module. It is whether ATU intends to be one of the institutions that defines what *Directing AI* *is* as a discipline — in Ireland, in Europe, internationally. The window is open. It will not be open in 2030.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">What *Directing AI* is</span>

*Directing AI* is the discipline of directing AI systems, human specialists, and the orchestrator's own judgement toward outcomes the orchestrator never executes themselves. It is a practice discipline — like architecture, surgery, or law — meaning it cannot be lectured into people and must be developed through supervised practice, repeated critique, and reflective work on real situations. It sits on top of domain expertise rather than replacing it: a clinical orchestrator must still understand clinical practice, a legal orchestrator must still understand legal practice. What *Directing AI* adds is the discipline for working well *with* AI tools rather than being directed by them.

The discipline rests on three substantive intellectual contributions that have emerged over the past eighteen months and constitute the *Orchestrated Cognition* framework:

<span style="color:#34495E">**A typology of fuzz.**</span> Professional situations involving fuzzy goals are not all the same kind of fuzzy. The discipline identifies six recognisable kinds (under-specified, time-pressured, generative, adversarial, collective, personal-stakes), each rewarding different orchestration responses. The recognition skill — *which kind of fuzz am I in?* — is the entry-level competence and the prerequisite for everything else.

<span style="color:#34495E">**A pattern language.**</span> Building on Christopher Alexander's tradition, the discipline articulates orchestration as a vocabulary of patterns — *Articulate the Fuzz*, *Verify-Before-Cite*, *Tool as Lens Not Oracle*, *Triangulate Across Positions*, and others — each a response to specific kinds of fuzz, each with documented forces resolved, characteristic applications, and known failure modes. The pattern catalogue is the discipline's working textbook in a real sense; it is what students engage with and what graduates contribute back to.

<span style="color:#34495E">**A spine anti-pattern: Borrowed Authority.**</span> AI tooling introduces a characteristic failure mode that older professional disciplines have long guarded against in their own ways: letting a tool's confident output substitute for a judgement only the orchestrator can make. The discipline names this explicitly, builds reflective practice against it, and treats resistance to *Borrowed Authority* as the most durable competence its graduates carry forward.

The programme name — *Directing AI* — asserts the framework's central claim grammatically and unambiguously: the human directs the AI. That grammatical commitment is not decoration. It is the discipline's first principle.

These three contributions are sufficient to constitute a teachable discipline. Together with the curriculum architecture across four credentialing tiers, they are sufficient to constitute the foundation of a recognised academic field — and ATU is positioned to walk the path from one to the other.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Why a three-year window</span>

New academic disciplines form in recognisable phases. They begin as practice traditions; they acquire conceptual vocabulary; they generate foundational artefacts (textbooks, catalogues, case libraries); they earn institutional recognition (modules, programmes, degrees, departments); they produce their own academic infrastructure (conferences, journals, PhD programmes, professional bodies); and they become foundational to surrounding disciplines.

The institutions that emerge as defining for a new field are almost always those that move during the artefact-generation and first-doctoral-cohort phases. Computer science has Stanford and MIT not because they invented computing but because they wrote the foundational textbooks and trained the first generations of academic computer scientists in the late 1960s and 1970s, when the discipline was still being shaped. Cybersecurity has Carnegie Mellon, UCL, and a handful of others for the same reason in a more recent generation. Cyberpsychology — a smaller discipline but a relevant precedent — has the institutions that ran the first MSc programmes and wrote the first textbooks; ATU is one of these.

*Directing AI* is currently in its artefact-generation phase. No canonical textbook exists. The pattern catalogue does not exist in publishable form outside emerging work. The case library is fragmentary. No conference exists. No journal exists. No PhD programmes exist. By 2029 or 2030, these will exist, and the institutions that produced them will be the institutions other universities cite, recruit faculty from, and benchmark against. After 2029, joining the defining group requires displacing institutions that arrived first — which is dramatically harder than arriving on time.

The three-year window is the period during which ATU can move from *building a portfolio of micro-credentials* to *producing the discipline's foundational artefacts and credentialing its first doctoral graduates*. After that, ATU can still run good courses in the discipline, but those courses will be teaching from other institutions' textbooks, contributing to other institutions' catalogues, and recruiting students who chose ATU because it was geographically convenient rather than because ATU defined the field.

International landscape: several major institutions are visibly experimenting with AI-pedagogy programmes, but most are still organised around tool fluency or framework training rather than around *Directing AI* as a substantive discipline. The early disorganisation of the international response is precisely what makes the window real. It will not stay disorganised.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">The ten-year trajectory</span>

The development of the discipline can be projected with reasonable confidence across four phases over the next decade. The phases are not predictions; they are the *shape* the discipline's growth will take if it follows the pattern established by analogous fields.

### <span style="color:#16A085">Phase 1 (2026–2028): Foundation and First Doctoral Cohort</span>

The discipline acquires its first credentialed programmes at every tier (undergraduate minor, Postgraduate Certificate, MSc, PhD), its first published pattern catalogue, and its first textbook. First cohorts of formally trained orchestrators graduate from the PG Cert. First MSc cohort begins. First PhD students enrol. The discipline becomes legible to outsiders for the first time. Universities that move in this phase establish the artefacts other universities will cite.

ATU's portfolio at the entry to this phase: undergraduate minor designed (Years 1–4, 55 ECTS, NFQ Levels 6/7/8), Level 9 Postgraduate Certificate designed (three 10-ECTS courses, 30 ECTS total), MSc structure designed (90 ECTS, hybrid taught and research), PhD programme architecture established (research questions identified across five families), case library at draft depth (six anchor cases articulated), pattern catalogue at draft depth (twenty-five starter patterns and twelve anti-patterns named with one-line descriptions), curriculum design and strategic case documents written.

What needs to be added by end of phase: all four credentialing tiers approved and running (or at least PG Cert and undergraduate minor running, with MSc launched and first PhD students enrolled); pattern catalogue v1 published in canonical form; case library at publishable depth; textbook v1 in circulation (online, living, modular); first PG Cert cohort entering practice; first MSc cohort entering research dissertations; first two or three PhD students at Year 1 confirmation.

### <span style="color:#16A085">Phase 2 (2028–2030): Consolidation and First Doctorates</span>

The discipline acquires its first formal academic infrastructure. First conferences are established. The faculty pool grows from the first cohorts of MSc graduates. A discipline-recognising body forms (initially informal — a steering committee, a working group, a society in formation). First MSc cohorts publish dissertations as recognised contributions to the discipline. The case for a primary undergraduate degree becomes institutionally credible. The first or second PhD candidates approach completion.

ATU's required posture in this phase: MSc programme running at sustained cohort size; doctoral programme producing first defences (2029–2030); convening role in any emerging Irish or European disciplinary body; first ATU faculty post explicitly designated for *Directing AI* (not borrowed from computing or psychology); engagement with the international discipline as a contributor rather than a consumer; publication of textbook v2 incorporating two cohorts' worth of pedagogical learning.

### <span style="color:#16A085">Phase 3 (2030–2033): Institutionalisation</span>

A primary undergraduate honours degree in *Directing AI* becomes feasible and is offered at the institutions that moved early. The first PhD graduates take up academic positions. Departments form. The discipline acquires a peer-reviewed journal of record and a recognised international conference. Other professional disciplines begin requiring *Directing AI* competence as a graduation criterion.

ATU's required posture: launch of *BSc (Honours) in Directing AI* as primary degree; first PhD supervisions completed; departmental formation, however small; first ATU PhD graduate appointed to academic post (within ATU or externally) marking the institution's discipline-producing capacity. The undergraduate minor restructures — possibly narrowing or splitting, with general-literacy components embedded into every ATU degree, and the serious specialist work moving to the primary degree.

### <span style="color:#16A085">Phase 4 (2033–2036): Maturity</span>

The discipline is internationally recognised, has its own academic society, its own foundational textbooks (multiple editions and competing schools), its own characteristic conferences, and its own positions across most research universities. *Directing AI* competence is foundational to professional education across many disciplines. The institutions that defined the discipline are visible and benchmarked against; the institutions that arrived late are playing catch-up they will not finish in this decade.

If ATU has moved well through Phases 1–3, it sits in the first group. If it has not, it sits in the second.

This trajectory is compressed relative to historical disciplines (computing took thirty years for the same arc). Three factors compress it: the speed of artefact dissemination on the internet, the size of the practitioner pool relative to the academic pool, and the unusually high industry demand creating pressure for graduates. The compressed trajectory is real and is not slowing.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">What ATU has already</span>

The discipline-building work currently in hand at ATU, in concrete artefacts rather than aspiration:

<span style="color:#34495E">**Portfolio of programme designs across four tiers.**</span> Undergraduate minor (six modules, four years, 55 ECTS). Postgraduate Certificate (three Level 9 courses, 30 ECTS). MSc (90 ECTS hybrid taught-and-research, structure designed). PhD programme architecture (research questions identified, supervisory model sketched, scholarship strategy needed). Individual brief documents for each tier. Coherent articulation between tiers.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Conceptual scaffolding (the *Orchestrated Cognition* framework).**</span> The fuzz typology (six kinds), the pattern language framing (drawing on Alexander, Schön, the studio tradition), the *Borrowed Authority* spine anti-pattern. These are the discipline's conceptual core, articulated at publishable depth in the portfolio documents.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Case library nucleus.**</span> Six anchor cases (Niamh, Cormac, Saoirse, Aoife, Ronan, Méabh) articulated as the discipline's working teaching text. Each case is currently at brief-document depth but is designed to be expanded into full case-library entries.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Pattern catalogue nucleus.**</span> Approximately twenty-five starter patterns and twelve anti-patterns named, with one-line descriptions and assignment to fuzz types. The catalogue is at sketch depth and requires substantial writing to reach publishable form, but the structural work has been done.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Pedagogical model.**</span> The studio-and-critique model adapted for undergraduate (case-method-led), postgraduate (master-critique-led), MSc (research-supervised), and PhD (research-independent) contexts. Assessment philosophy designed around production, peer critique, and public defence rather than examination. Explicit grounding in three named traditions (Alexander, Schön, the studio tradition).

<span style="color:#34495E">**Doctoral research questions.**</span> Five families of research questions identified (empirical, theoretical, pedagogical, applied, critical), each sufficient to absorb multiple PhD candidates. The doctoral programme has substantive intellectual content from day one rather than waiting for it to emerge.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Institutional alignment.**</span> ATU's existing micro-credential infrastructure, NFQ alignment, and Postgraduate Certificate framework — all of which fit the discipline's stacking model better than would the offerings of larger universities tied to single-degree structures. ATU's scale also permits moving at the speed the moment requires.

This is substantially more material than most institutions have when they begin establishing a new discipline. It is not yet sufficient. The gap between what exists and what a defining position requires is the work of the next eighteen to thirty-six months.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">What ATU needs to commit to next</span>

To convert the existing portfolio into a defining position, eight commitments are required across the 2026–2028 phase. Each is concrete enough to plan against and substantial enough to require strategic decision rather than operational follow-through.

<span style="color:#34495E">**1. Programme approval through ATU's quality processes for all four tiers.**</span> Undergraduate minor, PG Cert, MSc, and PhD must each move from designed-portfolio state to approved-and-scheduled state. Realistic timeline: PG Cert first cohort enrolling in autumn 2027; MSc launched alongside or shortly after, with first cohort autumn 2027 or 2028; undergraduate minor first cohort enrolling autumn 2028; first PhD students recruited and confirmed during 2028–2029. Each requires formal proposal development against ATU's templates appropriate to its level and movement through Academic Council. Begin Q3 2026; complete by 2028.

<span style="color:#34495E">**2. Authoring of foundational artefacts.**</span> The pattern catalogue, case library, and textbook (in the modern living-online sense rather than the traditional print sense) must be authored to publishable depth and released under open licences. Realistic timeline: pattern catalogue v1 published Q3 2027; case library expanded version Q1 2028; integrated textbook v1 Q3 2028. This is substantive writing work and is the discipline-defining contribution; ATU's name is on the canonical artefacts if they emerge from ATU.

<span style="color:#34495E">**3. Discipline namespace and infrastructure.**</span> The discipline acquires a *location* — a public-facing presence (domain, organisation, citation infrastructure) that other institutions can point at as the canonical reference. The pattern catalogue and case library live there. Citation conventions are established there. The textbook is hosted there. Low cost, high strategic value, requires deciding now rather than later.

<span style="color:#34495E">**4. Faculty development across multiple capacity tiers.**</span> Teaching at undergraduate and PG Cert level requires faculty who have *practised* the discipline. Supervising MSc research dissertations requires faculty who can *direct* research in the discipline. Supervising PhD work requires faculty who *are* researchers in the discipline — which means publishing their own work. Three to five ATU faculty initially across multiple schools, with explicit time and developmental support. Begin immediately; teaching capacity must exist by 2027.

<span style="color:#34495E">**5. Doctoral scholarship strategy.**</span> The PhD programme exists nominally without funded students; it functions only with them. ATU must develop an active strategy for attracting Irish Research Council, Science Foundation Ireland, industry-sponsored, and ATU-internal scholarship support for at least two PhD students per cohort by 2028. Without scholarship support, the doctoral tier remains a structural commitment with no operational reality.

<span style="color:#34495E">**6. Convening role.**</span> ATU should host the first ATU-led event in *Directing AI* — a workshop, symposium, or short conference — by autumn 2027. The event need not be large. Its function is to mark ATU as a place where the discipline is being thought about seriously, to bring practitioners and academics into contact with the ATU portfolio, and to begin building the external network that the discipline needs to grow.

<span style="color:#34495E">**7. Research output development.**</span> Senior academic contributors (initially primarily the lead author, then the first faculty cohort) must produce peer-reviewed publications, conference papers, and other research outputs in the discipline beginning in 2026. Without this, ATU teaches the discipline but is not seen to *advance* it. The first foundational publications can be drawn from existing portfolio material; subsequent publications require deliberate research planning.

<span style="color:#34495E">**8. Strategic resourcing decision.**</span> The work above is real work and cannot be done by one person on weekends. ATU should make a deliberate decision about resourcing — time allocation for the lead author, support for faculty development, modest budget for the convening event, infrastructure costs for the namespace and hosting, scholarship contribution for doctoral cohorts, support for publication and conference attendance. The cost is not high in absolute terms; the strategic value depends on doing the work rather than aspiring to it.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Why ATU specifically</span>

Several factors position ATU unusually well for this opportunity.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Scale.**</span> ATU is small enough to move faster than larger institutions. Programme approval, faculty development, and strategic commitment cycles that take three years at a major university can take eighteen months at ATU if institutional will is present. In a three-year window, that difference is decisive.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Infrastructure fit.**</span> The discipline's natural credentialing shape — micro-credentials at multiple levels, stackable to larger awards, designed for lifelong learning — fits ATU's strategic emphasis on micro-credentials and NFQ-stacked progression better than it fits any traditional research university's offerings.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Existing portfolio.**</span> The substantive design work for the foundational programmes already exists at ATU across all four credentialing tiers. No competing institution is starting from a comparable foundation; most are deciding whether to launch a single AI module while ATU has a coherent four-tier programme designed from undergraduate study through PhD.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Geographical and linguistic positioning.**</span> ATU sits in an English-speaking institution within the European Union, well-positioned to recruit from Ireland, the UK, and EU markets. The discipline's emergence is happening primarily in English; ATU's location is an advantage rather than an obstacle.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Existing strengths.**</span> ATU's established work in cyberpsychology, software development, and applied professional education provides natural intersection points with *Directing AI*. The discipline does not require ATU to start from nothing; it requires ATU to recognise that several existing strengths converge on it.

<span style="color:#34495E">**A lead author already in place.**</span> The portfolio work is being done. The continuation work is plannable. The dependency on one individual is real and must be addressed through faculty development, but the starting condition is unusually favourable.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Risks and dependencies</span>

The case for action is strong. The risks of action are real and must be named.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Author bandwidth.**</span> The foundational writing work currently depends on one individual whose primary academic commitments are completing a Master's dissertation through June 2026. Post-viva availability is genuine but finite. Mitigation: faculty development beginning in 2026 distributes the load across multiple academics by 2027; first MSc graduates begin contributing to the catalogue and case library through their dissertations from 2028.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Supervisory capacity.**</span> The doctoral tier requires PhD supervisors who are themselves researchers in the discipline. Initial supervisory capacity will be small (one or two academics able to lead-supervise; one or two more able to co-supervise). The doctoral programme can only grow at the rate supervisory capacity grows, and supervisory capacity grows partly through the doctoral programme itself. This is a real bootstrapping challenge requiring patience in cohort growth.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Discipline emergence timing.**</span> The three-year window is the central strategic assumption. If the discipline emerges faster than projected, the window closes before ATU can move; if slower, ATU's commitment looks premature. The compressed-timeline analysis is the better bet, but it is a bet.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Institutional approval cycles.**</span> ATU's programme approval processes, like those of all degree-awarding institutions, take time. The PG Cert can launch by autumn 2027 if approval work begins in Q3 2026; the MSc and PhD will likely require longer cycles and depend on whether ATU can run approvals in parallel rather than sequentially.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Competing institutional moves.**</span> Other Irish and European institutions will move. The question is not whether ATU faces competition; it is whether ATU moves first in those areas of the discipline where being first matters. The pattern catalogue, case library, and first PhD theses are once-only land grabs.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Pedagogical validation.**</span> The studio-and-critique model, master critique at scale, and the cohort-cap economics have not yet been tested in ATU's specific context. First-cohort delivery will reveal whether the design holds. Mitigation: deliberate small first cohorts in 2027, with explicit learning loops to feed into subsequent cohorts.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Resourcing.**</span> The work is plannable and the costs are modest by institutional standards, but they are real. Without explicit resourcing decision, the work happens slowly enough that the window closes. Doctoral scholarship support is a particular dependency — without funded doctoral students, the doctoral tier exists nominally only.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Decision points</span>

To act within the three-year window, the following decisions must be made on the following timeline.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Q3 2026.**</span> Strategic commitment decision at Academic Council level: is ATU pursuing *Directing AI* as a discipline-defining commitment across all four credentialing tiers, or as a portfolio of micro-credentials? The decision shapes everything downstream. If commitment is made, formal programme approval work begins immediately for all tiers in parallel.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Q4 2026.**</span> Resourcing decision: what time allocation, faculty development support, infrastructure budget, scholarship contribution, and publication support will be made available across 2027 and 2028? Decision on namespace and infrastructure (domain, organisation, hosting). Identification of initial faculty cohort (three to five academics) for development.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Q1–Q2 2027.**</span> Formal programme proposals for Postgraduate Certificate and MSc completed and submitted to Academic Council. Undergraduate minor proposal in development. PhD programme architecture document prepared for academic governance approval. Foundational writing work proceeds in parallel. First convening event planning begins.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Q3 2027.**</span> First ATU convening event in *Directing AI*. Pattern catalogue v1 published. First PG Cert cohort enrolling. MSc programme approved and prepared for first cohort. Undergraduate minor proposal submitted.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Q1 2028.**</span> Undergraduate minor approved. PhD programme approved and first scholarship applications begin. First MSc cohort enrolling. First PG Cert cohort completing.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Q3 2028.**</span> Textbook v1 published. First undergraduate minor cohort enrolling. First MSc cohort moving into dissertation phase. First PhD students enrolling subject to scholarship outcomes. ATU's position as a discipline-contributing institution is established.

<span style="color:#34495E">**2029–2030.**</span> First MSc dissertations published. First PhD students at Year 1 confirmation. ATU named in international conversations about the discipline.

<span style="color:#34495E">**2030–2033.**</span> First PhD theses defended. Primary undergraduate degree case made and approved. ATU's defining-institution status established or not.

These dates are aggressive but achievable if strategic commitment is made in 2026. They are not achievable if strategic commitment is deferred to 2027 — the window will partially close before ATU is positioned to act, and ATU will be teaching from other institutions' textbooks and recruiting PhD students for the discipline who chose other institutions because they got there first.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">The strategic question</span>

The portfolio work already done is substantial. It is, on its own, sufficient to run good programmes at ATU across the credentialing tiers. Running good programmes is a worthwhile outcome and does not require the strategic commitment this document calls for.

The strategic commitment is to a larger ambition: that ATU is not merely a place where *Directing AI* is taught, but one of the places where *Directing AI* is *defined*. That ambition requires the foundational artefacts to be authored at ATU, the first PhD theses to be defended at ATU, the discipline's location to live at ATU, the convening role to be held by ATU, and the international identity of the discipline to include ATU as one of its names.

This ambition is achievable within the three-year window. It is not achievable without explicit strategic commitment. The case for committing is the case made in this document. The case against committing is the cost of resourcing the work seriously, and the institutional risk of pursuing an ambitious position rather than a safe one.

The window does not stay open. The decision is whether ATU intends to walk through it.
