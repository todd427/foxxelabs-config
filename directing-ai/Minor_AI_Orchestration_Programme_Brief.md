# <span style="color:#1B4F72">Minor in AI Orchestration</span>
### <span style="color:#16A085">A four-year undergraduate programme in working with AI under uncertainty</span>

<span style="color:#7F8C8D">**Programme brief — May 2026** · Author: Todd McCaffrey</span>
<span style="color:#7F8C8D">**Structure:** Six modules · 55 ECTS · Years 1–4 of an honours undergraduate degree · NFQ Levels 6/7/8</span>
<span style="color:#7F8C8D">**Position:** Cross-faculty minor available to students of any primary undergraduate programme, starting in Year 1</span>
<span style="color:#7F8C8D">**Articulation:** Graduates progress naturally to *Reading the Fuzz* (Level 9) later in their careers</span>

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">What the programme is</span>

The *Minor in AI Orchestration* is a four-year undergraduate programme that develops the discipline of orchestrating AI systems against fuzzy professional goals, starting in the student's first weeks at university and scaffolded across the full duration of their primary degree. Students from any primary discipline can take the minor alongside their primary programme, graduating with an additional credential that signals demonstrable competence in AI orchestration thinking and four years of accumulated reflective practice.

The programme exists because the professional world current undergraduates are entering is already AI-orchestrated, and because the habits they form in Year 1 of university are the habits they will carry into their careers. A programme that begins in Year 2 implicitly accepts that students will spend their formative academic year using AI tools without a framework for how to think about them — installing the failure modes the programme exists to dismantle. This programme refuses that compromise. It starts in Week 1 of Year 1, when the habits are forming.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Why Year 1 matters most</span>

The single most durable thing the programme produces is the *Borrowed Authority* discipline — the habit of refusing to defer to a tool's confident output when judgement should be the orchestrator's. This habit cannot be installed by lecture; it has to be cultivated through reflective practice, repeated over years, until it becomes automatic.

A student who starts the reflective journal in Year 1 has *four years* of accumulated detection moments by the time they graduate. A student who starts in Year 2 has three. A student who starts in Year 4 has none worth speaking of — they have a concept rather than a habit. The difference between four years of practice and one is the difference between a discipline and a course.

Year 1 is also when the student is establishing how they use AI tools in their primary academic work. If the programme begins in Year 1, that primary academic work *is* the first orchestration practice — they learn to articulate the fuzz of an essay brief, to use AI tools as lenses rather than oracles when researching, to catch themselves outsourcing their thinking to a confident-sounding output. By Year 2, AI orchestration is something they *do* every day in their academic life, not something they study.

The programme treats Year 1 as the most important year. The other years build on it. Skipping it would be like teaching a music degree that didn't start until the third year, on the grounds that students don't yet know enough music to begin.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Programme structure</span>

Six modules across four years, totalling 55 ECTS. Students enter the minor in Year 1 of their primary degree.

### <span style="color:#16A085">Year 1 — Foundations and Reflective Practice</span>

<span style="color:#34495E">**Module 1: *Working with AI — First Principles* (5 ECTS, Level 6, Semester 1).**</span>

The entry module. Light credit load (5 ECTS, roughly 100–125 hours over the semester) so it sits comfortably alongside the student's primary first-semester programme. The module does four things: it builds deliberate, examined AI tool fluency in students who arrive with informal fluency; it introduces the concept of orchestration as a professional discipline; it presents the six anchor cases as stories of what professional work under uncertainty actually looks like; and it starts the reflective journal practice that runs through every subsequent module.

The pedagogy is seminar-and-workshop, with weekly small-group discussions of cases and tools. Assessment: workshop participation, three short reflective pieces, one introductory orchestration exercise (constructed brief, designed to be doable by a first-year undergraduate), and the start of the reflective journal.

The module is also deliberately *useful to the student's primary academic work*. Students leave it knowing how to articulate what they're uncertain about when starting an essay, how to use AI tools as research lenses rather than as essay-writers, and how to catch themselves outsourcing their thinking. They become better students in their primary discipline as a direct result of taking the module.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Module 2: *Reflection on Your Own AI Use* (5 ECTS, Level 6, Semester 2).**</span>

The reflective practice module. The student continues the journal from Module 1 and develops it into substantive reflective work — documenting their AI tool use across their primary academic programme, identifying *Borrowed Authority* moments as they catch them, articulating the fuzz of their academic assignments before they begin, and reflecting on what the discipline is changing in how they work.

This module is unusual in that it is *almost entirely reflective writing*. There are no examinations, no large projects, no group work. The student writes about their own practice, week by week, and the module's structure supports the depth of that reflection. Tutor critique runs throughout: each fortnight the tutor reads recent journal entries and provides written response, treating the journal as serious developmental work rather than as a diary.

Assessment: weekly journal entries (60%), three substantive reflective essays drawn from the journal (30%), final reflective synthesis at the end of the year (10%).

Why this is a credit-bearing module rather than an ongoing co-curricular activity: because it requires the student to take it seriously, and because reflective practice at this depth is real intellectual work that deserves recognition. ATU's tradition of recognising reflective practice as academic work (visible in nursing, education, and other practice-based disciplines) provides the institutional precedent.

### <span style="color:#16A085">Year 2 — Recognition</span>

<span style="color:#34495E">**Module 3: *Reading Fuzzy Situations* (10 ECTS, Level 6/7, Semester 1).**</span>

The recognition module. By Year 2 the student has a year of vocabulary, baseline tool fluency, and one year of reflective practice in their pocket. They are now ready to take recognition seriously.

The module teaches the six fuzz types in depth, the skill of reading hybrid situations, the practice of articulating fuzz precisely, and the discipline of catching framing capture. *Borrowed Authority* is named explicitly here as a programme spine and is engaged at a depth the Year 1 modules could not support. The pedagogy mixes case-method seminars with structured articulation exercises drawn from cases across professional domains. Assessment: fuzz portfolio, articulated-fuzz documents, *Borrowed Authority* detection log (drawing on a full year of journal entries), recognition test (case-based, not multiple-choice).

This module corresponds roughly to Movement I of the Level 9 *Reading the Fuzz* course, taught at undergraduate stakes with substantially more scaffolding.

### <span style="color:#16A085">Year 3 — Patterns and Composition</span>

<span style="color:#34495E">**Module 4: *Patterns of AI Orchestration* (10 ECTS, Level 7, Semester 1).**</span>

The patterns module. Students work through the orchestration pattern catalogue, learning patterns and anti-patterns as responses to specific kinds of fuzz. Each pattern is studied through worked examples in multiple domains. Students practise pattern application through small composition exercises in pairs and small groups. The pedagogy mixes seminar (pattern study and critique) with studio sessions (composition work). Assessment: pattern application exercises, peer composition critiques, pattern documentation in Alexandrian template, contribution to the cohort's pattern catalogue.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Module 5: *Orchestration Studio I* (10 ECTS, Level 7, Semester 2).**</span>

The first studio module. Students take on a substantial group orchestration project against a constructed but realistic brief from a partner organisation, building a working orchestration system over the semester, with weekly studio sessions and master critique throughout. The brief is constructed (not real-stakes) because Year 3 students are not yet ready for real-stakes practice, but it is designed to be credible and to require all the discipline they have learned to date.

Assessment: project artefacts, peer critique of other groups' work, final group defence in front of cohort, lecturer, and one invited industry mentor.

### <span style="color:#16A085">Year 4 — Integration and Capstone</span>

<span style="color:#34495E">**Module 6: *Orchestration Studio II — Capstone* (15 ECTS, Level 8, full year).**</span>

The honours-level integration. Students undertake a substantial individual orchestration project, either as a co-deliverable alongside their primary-degree capstone or, with permission of their primary programme, as a substantial component of it. The capstone runs over both semesters of Year 4, with monthly studio reviews and a public defence at the end.

The brief is the student's own — they propose a real or constructed orchestration problem from their primary discipline, agree it with the lecturer, and execute it with full pattern application, articulated fuzz, four years of documented *Borrowed Authority* detection brought to bear, and a defended final outcome. The defence is in front of cohort, lecturers, and two invited external critics per student drawn from industry, regulators, and academia.

By Year 4 the student has been working with the discipline for four years. The capstone draws on that depth in a way that no shorter programme could support.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Cross-cutting through the programme</span>

Three threads run through all six modules and across all four years:

<span style="color:#34495E">**The reflective journal.**</span> Started in Module 1, deepened in Module 2 as the primary assessed work, continued through every subsequent module as ongoing practice. By graduation the student has four full years of weekly reflective entries documenting their development. This artefact is the programme's most distinctive feature and the most durable thing the student carries forward.

<span style="color:#34495E">**The pattern catalogue.**</span> A living, versioned catalogue students engage with from Module 1 (encountering the first patterns as concepts) through Module 6 (contributing new patterns or revisions as part of their capstone). Shared with the Level 9 programme.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Cross-domain exposure.**</span> The cohort is drawn from multiple primary disciplines, and cases studied across the programme are drawn from many professional domains. By graduation a student has worked through orchestration problems in clinical, legal, policy, creative, business, and personal contexts.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Pedagogical methods across the programme</span>

<span style="color:#34495E">**Case-method seminars**</span> are the primary teaching mode in Modules 1, 3, and 4.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Reflective practice**</span> is primary in Module 2 and continues throughout. Module 2's structure — credit-bearing reflective writing with tutor critique — gives the practice its institutional weight.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Studio sessions**</span> appear in Module 4 (small) and become primary in Modules 5 and 6. The studio model is adapted from architectural training.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Peer critique**</span> is built into every module from Module 3 onward as a graded component.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Public defence**</span> appears at the end of Modules 5 and 6. Students defend their work in front of external critics twice — once in groups and once individually.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Assessment philosophy</span>

The programme uses no examinations across any of its six modules. Assessment consists of produced work, reflective writing, peer critique, and public defence. This reflects the discipline: orchestration is a practice skill and practice skills cannot be assessed by examination. A student who graduates from the minor has demonstrated their competence repeatedly, in front of peers, tutors, and external critics, across four years.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Audience</span>

Undergraduates from any honours-degree programme at ATU who wish to graduate with a demonstrable competence in AI orchestration alongside their primary discipline. The minor is structurally appropriate for students from computing, business, psychology (including cyberpsychology), law, design, communications, health sciences, education, engineering, humanities, and social sciences. The cross-domain composition of the cohort is structurally important and is encouraged through admission practice.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Prerequisites</span>

Enrolment in any ATU honours undergraduate programme. The minor begins in Year 1, Semester 1, and does not assume prior university experience. Some prior exposure to AI tools is assumed (Module 1 builds deliberate fluency on top of the informal fluency most students arrive with). Module 1 includes refresher material for any student who needs it. No coding background required for the minor as a whole.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">What graduates carry forward</span>

Four years of working with AI orchestration as a discipline. A reflective journal of substantive intellectual depth, documenting their development from first year of university through honours graduation. A pattern catalogue contribution that lives in the discipline's shared resources. A defended capstone project that demonstrates substantive professional capability at the boundary of their primary discipline. A *Minor in AI Orchestration* on their transcript.

Most importantly: the *Borrowed Authority* discipline as a habit established at the beginning of their university lives and developed for four years. A graduate of this minor has spent their entire undergraduate career catching themselves deferring to confident-sounding output when judgement should have been theirs. That habit is the most durable thing the programme produces. It is also precisely what their employers, clients, patients, and societies will need from them.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Articulation with the Level 9 programme</span>

The undergraduate minor is the developmental foundation for the Level 9 *Orchestrated Cognition* programme. Graduates do not take *Reading the Fuzz* immediately — they need three to five years of professional practice in their primary discipline first, to build the domain depth, stakes-bearing experience, and critique muscle the Level 9 course requires. The minor produces graduates ready to *develop* into senior orchestrators through professional practice; the Level 9 programme refines them once that practice has matured.

This articulation matters institutionally. ATU graduates of the undergraduate minor become natural candidates for the Level 9 programme later in their careers, producing a coherent lifelong-learning pathway through one institution.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Future expansion</span>

The minor as designed is the foundation. Future expansion might include domain-specialisation electives in Year 3, a short stack within the minor for students who want partial exposure, a postgraduate diploma bridge between the minor and the Level 9 programme, and eventually a major-track honours degree in AI orchestration once the discipline has matured further.
