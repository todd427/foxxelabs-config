# <span style="color:#1B4F72">Orchestrating Under Fuzz</span>
### <span style="color:#16A085">The studio course in AI orchestration</span>

<span style="color:#7F8C8D">**Brief — May 2026** · Author: Todd McCaffrey · NFQ Level 9 · 10 ECTS · 10-week studio</span>
<span style="color:#7F8C8D">**Course 2 of the *Orchestrated Cognition* programme** · Prerequisite: *Reading the Fuzz* or demonstrated equivalent</span>

---

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">What the course teaches</span>

*Orchestrating Under Fuzz* is the studio course. Learners arrive with the recognition skill from *Reading the Fuzz* and spend ten weeks building the response capacity — the patterns of orchestration that match specific kinds of fuzz, applied to a real integration project from the learner's own professional domain, defended in front of external critics at the end.

The course is built around the principle that orchestration cannot be lectured into people. It has to be practised under master supervision, against real work, with real stakes, with peer critique as part of the discipline. Architectural studios, surgical residencies, and the writers' workshop have known this for a century. This course applies the same model to AI orchestration.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">The central claim</span>

Patterns of orchestration are not universal moves. Each pattern is a response to a specific kind of fuzz, with characteristic forces it resolves and characteristic failure modes when applied to the wrong kind. The orchestrator's working competence is the ability to read the situation, select the patterns that match, compose them into a working system, judge the system's output, and defend the result. None of this can be learned without doing it. All of it can be learned by doing it under critique.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">The pattern catalogue</span>

The course works from a living, versioned catalogue of orchestration patterns and anti-patterns. Selected examples:

<span style="color:#34495E">**Cross-cutting patterns:**</span> *Articulate the Fuzz*, *Question the Question*, *Tool as Lens Not Oracle*, *Diverse Generators Single Judge*, *Failure as Signal*.

<span style="color:#34495E">**For synthesis fuzz (Niamh):**</span> *Parallel Exploration Before Commitment*, *Convergence as Signal*, *Verify-Before-Cite*.

<span style="color:#34495E">**For time-pressured fuzz (Cormac):**</span> *Bounded Sub-Orchestration*, *Reversibility-First Decomposition*, *Tripwire Verification*, *Defensible Trail*.

<span style="color:#34495E">**For generative fuzz (Saoirse):**</span> *Resist Premature Closure*, *Material Talks Back*.

<span style="color:#34495E">**For adversarial fuzz (Aoife):**</span> *Read Against the Obvious*, *Residue is Where Truth Lives*.

<span style="color:#34495E">**For collective fuzz (Ronan):**</span> *Triangulate Across Positions*, *Negotiation as Part of the Work*.

<span style="color:#34495E">**For personal-stakes fuzz (Méabh):**</span> *Tools as Thinking Partners Not Judges*.

The catalogue is open. Graduates contribute revisions and new patterns; the catalogue grows cohort by cohort.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Course structure</span>

Ten weeks, divided into a pattern movement and a studio movement:

<span style="color:#34495E">**Weeks 1–4: Patterns of Response.**</span> One fuzz family per week. Each week introduces the patterns and anti-patterns load-bearing for that kind of work, with a small composition exercise applying them to a domain example. By the end of Week 4, learners have built four small composed systems and critiqued each other's work in pattern terms.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Week 5: Composition Critique.**</span> Each learner submits a working composed system addressing a fuzzy goal from their own domain, and produces written critiques of two peer compositions. Critique is graded as heavily as production.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Weeks 6–10: Studio Project.**</span> A real, non-trivial orchestration of the learner's choosing, against a real problem from their own work, with a real stakeholder.

- Week 6: Fuzz Reading and Decomposition (profile, articulated-fuzz statement, decomposition diagram)
- Week 7: First Build (walking skeleton, failing informatively)
- Week 8: Second Build (refactored toward patterns; learner names three patterns applied, one anti-pattern removed, one *Borrowed Authority* moment detected and disarmed)
- Week 9: Pre-Defence Critique (cohort-only critique session; final refinement)
- Week 10: Final Defence (40-minute public defence per learner in front of cohort and invited external critics)

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Assessment</span>

No examinations. Pattern Application Exercises (20%, distributed across Weeks 1–4) · Composition System and Critique (20%, Week 5) · Studio Decomposition (5%, Week 6) · Studio Builds (15%, Weeks 7–8) · Pattern Documentation (10%, one pattern in Alexandrian template) · Final Defence (20%, Week 10) · Cohort Catalogue Contribution (10%, peer-reviewed).

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">What graduates take away</span>

Working orchestration practice applied to their own domain. A documented studio project defended in front of external critics. Contributions to a living pattern catalogue under the programme's name. The discipline to compose AI orchestrations professionally — and to refuse to ship work that depends on *Borrowed Authority* rather than the orchestrator's own judgement. A Level 9 micro-credential at 10 ECTS, completing (with *Reading the Fuzz*) a 20-ECTS Level 9 sequence stackable toward a Postgraduate Certificate.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Audience</span>

Graduates of *Reading the Fuzz*, plus admitted-by-portfolio practitioners with demonstrated equivalent recognition competence. Mid-career professionals who exercise independent judgement in their domain and are ready to put orchestration into supervised, critiqued, real-stakes practice on a substantial project of their own choosing.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Prerequisites</span>

*Reading the Fuzz* completed, or admission by portfolio review demonstrating equivalent recognition competence — including a documented fuzz reading of a real piece of the applicant's professional work and at least one detected and articulated *Borrowed Authority* moment from their own practice. All other prerequisites from *Reading the Fuzz* apply.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Format</span>

NFQ Level 9 · 10 ECTS · 10-week studio · Cohort capped at 12–16 · Studio sessions plus independent build, critique, and project work · Master critique throughout · Final defence in front of cohort and invited external critics from industry, academia, and domain practice.
