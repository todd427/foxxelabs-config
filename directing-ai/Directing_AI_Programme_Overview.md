## <span style="color:#1B4F72">What it is</span>

*Directing AI* is a coherent, four-tier programme in the discipline of directing AI systems, human specialists, and the orchestrator's own judgement toward professional outcomes the orchestrator never executes themselves. The programme rests on the *Orchestrated Cognition* framework — the conceptual position that AI orchestration is a substantive practice discipline with its own typology, pattern language, and characteristic failure modes. The programme name asserts the framework's central claim: the human directs the AI, not the other way around.

The programme runs from Week 1 of an honours undergraduate degree through doctoral-level research, structured as four credentialing tiers with explicit articulation between them. It is built on the recognition that *Directing AI* is a *practice discipline* — like architecture, surgery, or law — that cannot be lectured into people and must be developed through supervised practice, reflective work, and sustained critique across years.

The programme exists because the professional world ATU's students are entering is already AI-orchestrated. Universities that teach *Directing AI* as a discipline produce graduates equipped to retain judgement, accountability, and authorship throughout their careers. Universities that treat AI as an optional module produce graduates whose habits form unsupervised — graduates who use the tools well or badly without a framework for the difference.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">The portfolio at a glance</span>

The programme runs across four credentialing tiers, totalling more than 175 ECTS of curriculum:

### <span style="color:#16A085">Undergraduate tier — *Minor in Directing AI*</span>

Six modules across four years of an honours undergraduate degree (any primary discipline). Cross-faculty elective. Total: 55 ECTS across NFQ Levels 6, 7, and 8.

| Year | Module | ECTS | Level |
|---|---|---:|---|
| 1 | Working with AI: First Principles | 5 | 6 |
| 1 | Reflection on Your Own AI Use | 5 | 6 |
| 2 | Reading Fuzzy Situations | 10 | 6/7 |
| 3 | Patterns of Directing AI | 10 | 7 |
| 3 | Directing AI Studio I | 10 | 7 |
| 4 | Directing AI Studio II — Capstone | 15 | 8 |

Graduates leave with the recognition vocabulary, the *Borrowed Authority* discipline as a habit, a four-year reflective journal, and a defended capstone project in their primary discipline.

### <span style="color:#16A085">Postgraduate Certificate tier — *Postgraduate Certificate in Directing AI*</span>

Three 10-ECTS courses at NFQ Level 9, totalling 30 ECTS. Studio model with master critique. Designed for mid-career professionals with domain depth.

| Course | ECTS | Cohort cap | Audience |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Reading the Fuzz | 10 | 20 | Senior practitioners |
| Orchestrating Under Fuzz | 10 | 12–16 | Practitioners (prereq: Reading the Fuzz) |
| Orchestrating at Scale | 10 | 10–12 | Senior leaders (prereq: Orchestrating Under Fuzz) |

Graduates leave with working orchestration practice, a defended studio project, contributions to the discipline's pattern catalogue, and a Level 9 Postgraduate Certificate.

### <span style="color:#16A085">Master's tier — *MSc in Directing AI*</span>

A hybrid taught-and-research master's degree, 90 ECTS at NFQ Level 9 over one calendar year full-time (or two part-time). The taught component is the Postgraduate Certificate plus additional advanced modules; the research component is a substantial supervised dissertation designed to contribute to the discipline's foundational corpus.

| Component | ECTS |
|---|---:|
| Postgraduate Certificate (three courses) | 30 |
| Research Methods for Directing AI Studies | 10 |
| Advanced Elective (domain specialisation or theory) | 10 |
| Dissertation (supervised, publishable contribution) | 40 |

Graduates leave with a research master's qualification in a forming discipline, a dissertation that contributes to the discipline's pattern catalogue, case library, or theoretical scaffolding, and the foundation to pursue doctoral work.

### <span style="color:#16A085">Doctoral tier — *PhD in Directing AI*</span>

Standard Irish doctoral structure at NFQ Level 10 (4 years full-time, 6–8 years part-time). Supervisory team of two — primary supervisor in the discipline, co-supervisor from a related field (computing, cyberpsychology, cognitive science, education, philosophy). Research questions across five families: empirical (how orchestrators actually work), theoretical (the structure of the discipline's frame), pedagogical (how *Directing AI* is acquired), applied (orchestration in specific domains), and critical (where the discipline fails and whose interests it serves).

The first PhD theses awarded at ATU in *Directing AI* establish ATU as a discipline-defining institution in a way no taught programme alone can. The doctoral tier is the discipline's research engine.

### <span style="color:#16A085">Articulation across tiers</span>

The four tiers form a coherent lifelong programme. The undergraduate minor establishes the recognition vocabulary and *Borrowed Authority* discipline at the start of professional formation. The Postgraduate Certificate refines those into working professional practice once domain expertise has matured. The MSc adds research capacity for graduates who want to contribute to the discipline rather than only practise it. The PhD produces the next generation of academic researchers who will advance the discipline beyond what the founders authored.

This articulation matters institutionally. ATU students who take the undergraduate minor become natural candidates for the PG Cert at mid-career, for the MSc when they want a research-bearing qualification, and for the PhD when they want academic standing. ATU recruits its own future cohorts at every level.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">The central move — Read the Fuzz Before Responding to It</span>

The programme teaches recognition first. Six anchor cases — composite figures drawn from real professional work — span the kinds of fuzzy professional situations directing AI actually encounters:

- <span style="color:#34495E">**Niamh**</span>, a policy analyst with four days to produce a position paper on a topic she has to define as she works — *under-specified fuzz with discoverable structure*
- <span style="color:#34495E">**Cormac**</span>, a consultant cardiologist taking a phone call at 11pm about a deteriorating patient — *time-pressured fuzz with irreversible action*
- <span style="color:#34495E">**Saoirse**</span>, a documentary director with 40 hours of footage and no film yet — *generative fuzz with no out-there answer*
- <span style="color:#34495E">**Aoife**</span>, a senior counsel preparing cross-examination from 4,000 pages against a witness who has been prepped — *adversarial fuzz with a moving counterparty*
- <span style="color:#34495E">**Ronan**</span>, a startup founder asking "what are we actually building?" with co-founders, team, and investors all holding different answers — *collective fuzz with self-implicating choice*
- <span style="color:#34495E">**Méabh**</span>, the 47-year-old daughter of an 81-year-old father after a possible TIA — *personal-stakes fuzz where tools shape the question*

Each kind of fuzz rewards different orchestration moves. The recognition skill — *which kind of fuzz am I in?* — is prior to every other skill the programme teaches.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">The spine — discipline against Borrowed Authority</span>

Across all six cases, a single failure mode recurs: *Borrowed Authority* — letting a tool's confident output substitute for a judgement only the human director can make. Professional disciplines have long guarded against this in their own ways: footnotes, peer review, second opinions, witness preparation, board minutes. AI tooling makes the failure easy and frictionless. The programme's discipline against Borrowed Authority runs through every tier — embedded in the undergraduate reflective journal, sustained through the PG Cert studio work, examined critically in the MSc dissertation, and investigated empirically and theoretically in PhD work. It is the orientation graduates carry forward into their professional lives for decades.

The programme name asserts the same commitment: the human *directs* the AI. The framework's central claim and the programme's identity converge in the title.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">The pedagogical frame</span>

The *Orchestrated Cognition* framework draws on three traditions, named explicitly:

<span style="color:#34495E">**Christopher Alexander**</span>'s pattern-language work — patterns as discovered rather than invented, the *quality without a name* as the criterion of design, the master-apprentice studio as the natural home for learning a felt discipline.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Donald Schön**</span>'s *reflection-in-action* — the practitioner thinking with the work as it unfolds, where goal and solution co-evolve and the work itself is the medium of learning.

The <span style="color:#34495E">**studio tradition**</span> as practised in architectural training, surgical residency, and the writers' workshop — real work, master critique, judgement cultivated through repeated exposure to one's own and others' failures.

Patterns are taught as responses to specific kinds of fuzz, not as universal moves. The pattern catalogue is versioned, open, and contributed back to by graduates. The pedagogy is practice-first across all tiers: learners build, fail, and critique under supervision. There are no examinations in any module or course at any tier.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">The strategic frame</span>

*Directing AI* is a forming academic discipline. Within the next three years, a small number of institutions internationally will produce the discipline's foundational artefacts — its first canonical textbook, its first published pattern catalogue, its first recognised case library, its first credentialed programmes at scale, and its first PhD theses. Those institutions will be cited, benchmarked against, and recognised as defining the field for the decade that follows.

ATU is positioned to be one of those institutions. The foundational design work for the programme is complete across all four tiers. The discipline's conceptual scaffolding has been articulated. The case library and pattern catalogue have begun emerging. ATU's scale permits movement at the speed the moment requires. The portfolio described in this document is not a proposal to enter a settled field; it is the foundation of a position in a discipline ATU can help to define.

The companion strategic document — *Directing AI as an Emerging Discipline: A Trajectory and Institutional Case for ATU* — sets out the ten-year projection for the discipline, the artefacts ATU needs to author over the next eighteen months, the four-tier credentialing trajectory, and the decision points the next twelve months must address. The programme described here is the credentialing layer of that larger strategic position.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Audience and reach</span>

The programme serves distinct audiences across its four tiers:

<span style="color:#34495E">**Undergraduates from any honours-degree programme at ATU.**</span> The minor is structurally appropriate for students from computing, business, psychology, law, design, communications, health sciences, education, engineering, humanities, and social sciences. Cross-domain cohort composition is structurally important.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Mid-career professionals returning for postgraduate credentials.**</span> The Postgraduate Certificate serves analysts, researchers, project leads, senior practitioners, regulators, and other professionals who exercise independent judgement in their domains. No coding background is required at this tier.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Senior leaders moving into directing-AI leadership.**</span> The third course of the Postgraduate Certificate (*Orchestrating at Scale*) serves heads of function, programme directors, founders, principals, and senior consultants whose work is at organisational scale.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Research-track candidates pursuing the MSc.**</span> Graduates of the PG Cert who wish to add research capacity to their professional practice; mid-career professionals seeking a research master's in a forming discipline; aspiring PhD candidates whose research interests fall in *Directing AI*.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Doctoral candidates pursuing the PhD.**</span> MSc graduates with strong research dissertations; mid-career practitioners with substantial professional standing who wish to contribute academically to the discipline. Scholarship support (IRC, SFI, industry partnerships, internal ATU funding) will be sought to enable doctoral cohorts.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Format and pedagogy</span>

All modules and courses at every tier use studio-based pedagogy with no examinations. The undergraduate tier mixes case-method seminars with studio work and sustained reflective journal practice. The Postgraduate Certificate tier uses master-critique studio with peer critique graded as heavily as production. The MSc adds research methods training and a substantial supervised dissertation. The PhD follows the standard Irish doctoral structure with structured first-year work and progressive independence.

Public defence in front of cohort and external critics features at each tier's culmination — capstone in the undergraduate minor, final defence in each PG Cert course, dissertation viva in the MSc, doctoral viva in the PhD.
