# Directing AI — Naming and Style

## Three-tier architecture

- **Discipline:** *Directing AI* — the credential name (Minor, PgCert, MSc, PhD in *Directing AI*).
- **Framework:** *Orchestrated Cognition* — the intellectual position the discipline rests on.
- **Courses:** *Reading the Fuzz*, *Orchestrating Under Fuzz*, *Orchestrating at Scale* — specific developmental moves.
- **Anti-pattern:** *Borrowed Authority* — embedded in the discipline name by putting the human in the subject position and AI in the object position.

Programme name plain on the surface and rich underneath; framework name evocative; course names characterful. Each tier does its proper job; no tier conflates roles.

## Why *Directing AI*

- Right verb. *Direct* is what an orchestrator actually does. Carries authority-with-judgement meaning.
- Professional gravity. *Director* is a senior title across many domains.
- Grammatically active and human-centred. Human in subject position, AI in object position — the *Borrowed Authority* discipline embedded in the name.
- Plain and confident. Two words, no decoration.
- Distinctive without being quirky. No clear prior claim.
- Two-word compound built from durable terms.

Rejected alternatives: *AI Orchestration* (becoming generic in industry), *Orchestrated Cognition* alone (retained as framework, not discipline name), various longer compounds.

## Italicisation convention

- Italicise *Directing AI* as a proper noun (the discipline name). Do **not** italicise "directing AI" as a verb phrase.
- Same convention for *Orchestrated Cognition* (framework), *Borrowed Authority* (anti-pattern), course names (*Reading the Fuzz*, *Orchestrating Under Fuzz*, *Orchestrating at Scale*), and pattern names.
- Within module names in tables (e.g. "Patterns of Directing AI"), do **not** italicise — the whole module name functions as the unit. Same convention as not italicising "Business Administration" inside "Master of Business Administration".
- In document title headings the discipline name is plain (it IS the title); in body text it gets italicised as a proper-noun reference.
