# <span style="color:#1B4F72">Orchestrating at Scale</span>
### <span style="color:#16A085">Leading AI orchestration in organisations</span>

<span style="color:#7F8C8D">**Brief — May 2026** · Author: Todd McCaffrey · NFQ Level 9 · 10 ECTS · 10-week studio</span>
<span style="color:#7F8C8D">**Course 3 of the *Orchestrated Cognition* programme** · Completes the Postgraduate Certificate (30 ECTS) · Prerequisite: *Orchestrating Under Fuzz* or demonstrated equivalent</span>

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">What the course teaches</span>

*Orchestrating at Scale* takes graduates of individual orchestration practice and prepares them to lead orchestration as an organisational discipline. The first two courses train the orchestrator. This course trains the person who builds the orchestration function — who designs accountability structures, sets standards, leads teams of orchestrators, interfaces with regulators and boards, and embeds the discipline into how their organisation works.

The shift is from *doing* orchestration well to *making it possible for others to do orchestration well at organisational scale*. That's a different skill, and it's the one most senior practitioners will need from a Level 9 credential.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">The central claim</span>

Orchestration at organisational scale is not just orchestration with more people. It is a substantively different discipline, with its own failure modes, its own pattern catalogue, and its own forms of *Borrowed Authority* — only now the borrowed authority is institutional rather than individual. An organisation can defer to its tooling, its consultants, its dashboards, or its "AI strategy" in exactly the way a single practitioner defers to a confident-sounding output, and the consequences are larger because the work is bigger and the accountability is more diffuse.

This course teaches the senior practitioner how to recognise and resist organisational *Borrowed Authority*, how to design systems that keep judgement loaded onto the right humans, and how to build orchestration discipline into the structures, processes, and culture of an organisation that has to ship work in front of clients, patients, juries, regulators, or boards.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">The four leadership problems</span>

The course is organised around the four problems senior orchestrators consistently face at scale:

<span style="color:#34495E">**Leading orchestrators.**</span> When the learner is no longer doing the orchestration themselves but directing others who are. Maintaining quality across multiple orchestrators working on related problems. Coaching judgement rather than dictating outputs. Knowing when to override a junior orchestrator's call and when to trust it. Building a critique culture rather than a compliance culture.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Governance and accountability.**</span> Building the accountability structures that keep *Borrowed Authority* out of organisational decisions. Audit trails for AI-assisted work. Sign-off procedures that load judgement onto the right humans. Interface with internal ethics functions, external regulators, professional bodies, boards. The institutional version of footnoting, peer review, and witness preparation.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Embedding the discipline.**</span> How orchestration practice gets into the day-to-day work of an organisation. Hiring criteria. Training and onboarding. Standards documents. Review processes. The slow cultural work that determines whether orchestration becomes a real practice or just another framework the leadership team is enthusiastic about.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Long-horizon orchestration.**</span> Problems that take months or years, where the goal will shift multiple times before completion. Multi-stakeholder programmes where the orchestrator is coordinating across teams, departments, or organisations. Maintaining recognition discipline when the fuzz changes over time.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Borrowed Authority at organisational scale</span>

The course's central professional discipline — resistance to *Borrowed Authority* — operates differently at scale. Institutional versions include:

- *Dashboard Authority*: deferring to whatever the AI-generated dashboard shows because it looks rigorous
- *Consultant Authority*: deferring to external advisers because they're external
- *Process Authority*: deferring to the documented process because it's documented, even when the process was designed for a different problem
- *AI-Strategy Authority*: deferring to the organisation's stated AI strategy as a substitute for situated judgement
- *Tooling Authority*: building the organisation around the affordances of its current tools rather than around the work it needs to do

Each is a form of the same underlying failure: substituting confident-looking institutional output for the judgement someone in the organisation should be making. The course teaches the senior practitioner to recognise each form, to design accountability structures that resist them, and to model the discipline in their own leadership practice.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Course structure</span>

Ten weeks, seminar-and-studio model, smaller cohort, deeper peer discussion than Courses 1 and 2:

<span style="color:#34495E">**Weeks 1–2: From Practice to Leadership.**</span> The shift from individual orchestrator to leader of orchestration. Case studies of senior practitioners and the failure modes they encounter. The four leadership problems introduced. Each learner writes a leadership-fuzz reading of their own current organisational position.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Weeks 3–4: Governance and Accountability.**</span> Building the institutional disciplines that resist organisational Borrowed Authority. Audit trails, sign-off, ethics review, regulatory interface. Cases from regulated industries (clinical, legal, financial) where these structures have been worked through over decades. Patterns for designing accountability into AI-orchestrated work.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Weeks 5–6: Leading Orchestrators.**</span> Coaching judgement. Building critique culture. Hiring orchestrators. Setting standards without dictating outputs. The senior orchestrator as the *master* in a master-apprentice tradition, made operational at organisational scale.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Weeks 7–8: Embedding the Discipline.**</span> Organisational change practice applied to orchestration. How orchestration gets into hiring, training, review processes, and culture. The slow work that determines whether orchestration becomes a real practice or a corporate slogan.

<span style="color:#34495E">**Weeks 9–10: Programme Defence.**</span> Each learner designs an orchestration programme for a real organisation — typically their own employer, a partner organisation, or an organisation they advise. The design covers governance, leadership structure, embedding strategy, and the first 18 months of execution. Public defence in front of cohort and invited senior critics from industry, regulators, and academia.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Assessment</span>

No examinations. Leadership Fuzz Reading (10%, Weeks 1–2) · Governance Design Exercise (15%, Weeks 3–4) · Coaching Cases (15%, Weeks 5–6) · Embedding Strategy (15%, Weeks 7–8) · Programme Design Document (20%, Week 10) · Programme Defence (15%, Week 10) · Catalogue Contribution (10%, peer-reviewed — typically a pattern at organisational scale).

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">What graduates take away</span>

A working leadership discipline for AI orchestration at organisational scale. A defended programme design they can take forward into their own organisation. Contributions to the pattern catalogue at the organisational-scale tier. Completion of a 30-ECTS Postgraduate Certificate in *Orchestrated Cognition* at NFQ Level 9 — a credential that signals not just individual practice but readiness to build and lead orchestration as an organisational function.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Audience</span>

Senior practitioners with demonstrated individual orchestration competence. Heads of function, programme directors, founders, principals, senior consultants, regulators, and those preparing to move into such roles. The course is designed for people whose work is at organisational scale, who are responsible for the orchestration practice of others as well as their own, and who need a credential that recognises that level of professional practice.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Prerequisites</span>

*Orchestrating Under Fuzz* completed, or admission by portfolio review demonstrating equivalent individual orchestration competence — including a defended studio project at scale comparable to the Course 2 final defence. Senior professional standing strongly preferred (typically 8+ years' post-qualification experience with leadership responsibility). All other prerequisites from the earlier courses apply.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Scope</span>

The course teaches orchestration leadership — the leadership of others doing orchestration, the design of accountability structures, and the embedding of the discipline in organisational practice. It does not teach individual orchestration (that is Courses 1 and 2), general leadership theory (other Level 9 offerings cover that), or AI ethics as a standalone subject (though ethics runs throughout). It is the completion of the Level 9 *Orchestrated Cognition* sequence, not a substitute for any of its earlier courses.

## <span style="color:#1B4F72">Format</span>

NFQ Level 9 · 10 ECTS · 10-week studio · Cohort capped at 10–12 (smaller than earlier courses to support deep peer discussion at leadership level) · Seminar and studio sessions · Master critique throughout · Final defence in front of cohort and senior external critics from industry, regulatory practice, and academia · Suitable as the third course of a Postgraduate Certificate in *Orchestrated Cognition* (30 ECTS at NFQ Level 9).
